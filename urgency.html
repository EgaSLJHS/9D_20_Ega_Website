The Clock Is Ticking

Borneo's ecosystem is facing a deadly crisis. Every minute, areas of the rainforest are destroyed.

01

Scale of Destruction

Deforestation in Borneo is happening at a huge scale. Since the 1970s, Borneo has lost over 30% of its rainforest, with some regions being reduced to half. From 2000-2015, roughly 6 million hectares of forest were cleared.

Over 2.6 million hectares across Indonesia and Borneo were burned. These fires are often intenionally set to clear land, but they spread into other places, releasing massive carbon dioxide.

Palm

Borneo now contains millions of hectares for palm oil trees, removing the plants and animals from their homes by chopping down native trees. This decreases biodiversity, causing many populations to boom or die, both outcomes disrupting the ecosystem.

What Is Happening

Entire rainforests are being cleared and burned. Ancient and dense tropical forests that took millions of years to form are being erased in seconds. What we have now is empty acres of dirt surrounded by foreign palm trees, which disrupts the life of plants, animals, and humans. Plants lose their source of life, animals lose their homes, while humans drown in floods.

Why It Is Happening

Deforestation is done to fulfill economic needs. Palm oil is needed in over 50% of supermarket products, making it one of the most used oils in the world. Clearing forests help provide more palm trees to fulfill this demands. logging industries also extract valuable hardwoods like meranti and ebony to sell, while the empty land produced is used for said palm trees and infrastructure.

  • Palm oil = low cost, high profit
  • Timber = valuable for sale
  • Land = used for agriculture

Why It Keeps Spreading

Deforestation expands because it of conditions that lead to more deforestation:

  • Seperated forests dry out, making them vulnerable to fires
  • Cleared land increases land value, encouraging further deforestation
  • Weak laws allow illegal logging to happen
  • High usage of palm oils drive companies to empty forests more

Effect On Flora

Borneo's forests are usually dominated by dipterocarp trees, which become the main part of the ecosystem. When these trees are removed, the entire ecosystem collapses.

  • Dipterocarps: Critical canopy trees that regulate light, temperature, and habitat structure. With it gone, the environment isn't suitable for life anymore, causing species to be unable to live
  • Rafflesia arnoldii: Its already rare population decline more as deforestation removes the vines they grow off of and the disruption of conditions for their growth
  • Orchids: Up to 80% of epiphytic orchids die when the trees they grow on get chopped, as they lose their structure and moisture.
  • Nepenthes (pitcher plants): Many species become threatened, as the soil dries and temperature increases within lands that have lost their trees.

Effect On Fauna

Borneo's animals depend on forest ecosystems, but deforestation causes their survival conditions to be disrupted. Habitat loss have causes a decrease in population among multiple species.

  • Bornean orangutan (Pongo pygmaues): Over 100,000 orangutans dies between 1999-2015 due to the forest loss which removes their food source (fruit trees) and isolates groups, leading to starvation and low reproduction rates.
  • Bornean pygmy elephant: Deforestation seperates habitats and block migration routes, forcing them to go into plantations and human civilization, arising conflict between them and humans which increase death.
  • Sunda clouded leopard: Population drops in stripped forests because it reduces there territory leading to smaller hunting success and breeding opportunities.
  • Hornbills: Deforestation removes nesting places in trees, and without them, breeding rates decline, causing the bird population to slowly disappear

Effect On Humans

Around 20 million people in Borneo depend on the rainforest for food, water, shelter, and income. When the forest is lost, daily life is disrupted too.

  • Indigenous communities: Forest loss removes access to food sources, plants, and materials for shelter while also pushing away communities from the land they live in.
  • Health impacts from haze: Fire used for deforestation sends toxic smoke into the air, increasing respiratory illness, eye irritation, and long-term health risks.
  • Loss of clean water: Soil erosion and river pollution contaminate water sources that people rely on to live.

Effect On Nature

The removal of forests does not only affect people and wildlife. It also damages the natural systems that keep the rainforest stable.

  • Flooding: Without tree roots to absorb water, rain rushes quickly to rivers and can cause sudden floods.
  • Soil erosion: Soil washes away when roots no longer hold the land together, making the ground less fertile and increasing landslides.
  • Fire: Cleared and dried land becomes highly flammable and can burn for months under hot conditions.
  • Climate impact: Burning forests release huge amounts of carbon dioxide, worsening global warming and disrupting rainfall patterns.
02

Why This Matters Now

The deforestation of Borneo's rainforest is a ongoing problem not one we can prevent in the future before it happens. This means that it is already affecting the world negatively through the removal of trees. And these negative effects can become deadly and even irreversible if we don't try to stop it. Without our actions, the climate would change, animals and plants would cease to exist, and life for us humans would be way harder.

  • Large areas of the rainforest will be lost forever without your funds to help us save it.
  • Without action, more chopped trees will increase the levels of carbon dioxide globally, making extreme weather appear frequently.
  • More and more animals like orangutans and pygmy elephants are already losing numbers due to deforestation.

At the current rate, deforestation without intervention may change the whole world as we know it.
